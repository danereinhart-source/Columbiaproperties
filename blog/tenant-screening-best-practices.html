Tenant Screening Best Practices for Arizona Landlords

May 21, 2026 • 10 min read

Selecting the right tenant is one of the most critical decisions you'll make as a Phoenix landlord. A reliable, responsible tenant means stable income, minimal maintenance issues, and a positive rental experience. Conversely, a problematic tenant can lead to late payments, property damage, legal complications, and extended vacancy periods.

At Columbia Properties, we've developed comprehensive tenant screening processes that help landlords identify quality tenants while remaining fully compliant with Arizona fair housing laws. In this guide, we'll share the best practices every Phoenix landlord should implement.

Why Thorough Tenant Screening Matters

The cost of a bad tenant can be substantial. According to industry data, evicting a problematic tenant can cost $3,000-$10,000 or more in legal fees, lost rent, and repairs. A tenant with a history of late payments might cost you thousands in unpaid rent. By investing time in proper screening, you reduce the risk of costly problems down the road.

The key is implementing a consistent, documented process that applies the same standards to all applicants. This protects you legally and ensures you're making objective decisions.

Step 1: Application and Income Verification

Start with a comprehensive rental application that collects essential information:

Application Information to Gather

  • Full legal name and Social Security number (for credit reporting authorization)
  • Current and previous addresses (last 3-5 years)
  • Current and previous landlords with contact information
  • Employment history and current employer details
  • Personal references (non-family members)
  • Information about occupants and any pets
  • Disclosures regarding criminal history and evictions

Income Verification

Verify that the applicant's income is sufficient to cover rent. A common standard is that monthly rent should not exceed 30% of gross monthly income. To verify income:

  • Request recent pay stubs (typically last 2-3 months)
  • Request proof of employment letter from employer
  • For self-employed applicants, request recent tax returns and bank statements
  • For other income sources (retirement, disability, etc.), request official statements
Pro Tip: Verify employment directly by calling the employer's HR department. Confirm the position, employment duration, and anticipated continuation of employment.

Step 2: Credit and Financial History Check

A credit check reveals an applicant's financial responsibility and payment history. Here's what to look for:

Credit Report Analysis

  • Payment history: Look for patterns of late payments, particularly for housing-related accounts
  • Collections accounts: Unpaid debts sent to collections indicate serious financial problems
  • Credit score: Most landlords prefer scores above 650-700, though standards vary
  • Eviction history: Previous evictions are serious red flags
  • Recent bankruptcy: Consider the age and circumstances of any bankruptcy filing

Using Credit Reporting Services

Use reputable third-party screening services that specialize in rental applicant screening. These services access credit reports, eviction history, and sometimes criminal records. Popular options include RentBureau, Experian, Equifax, and LexisNexis.

Remember that you must obtain the applicant's written consent before pulling their credit report, and you must follow Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA) regulations.

Step 3: Landlord Reference Checks

Previous landlords can provide invaluable insights into an applicant's rental history:

  • Payment history: Did they pay rent on time? How often were payments late?
  • Property condition: Did they maintain the property well? Were there damage issues?
  • Lease violations: Did they violate any lease terms or house rules?
  • Noise complaints: Were there complaints from neighbors?
  • Reason for moving: Why did they leave? Were they evicted or asked to leave?
  • Would they rent again: Would the landlord recommend them?
Important: Contact landlords directly rather than relying solely on applicant-provided references. Applicants may provide numbers for friends posing as landlords. Verify the contact is legitimate by looking up the property management company independently.

Step 4: Criminal Background Check

Arizona law allows landlords to conduct criminal background checks, but you must follow specific guidelines:

Legal Requirements

  • Obtain written consent from the applicant before conducting the check
  • You cannot automatically deny applicants based on criminal history alone
  • You must consider the nature of the crime, how long ago it occurred, and evidence of rehabilitation
  • You cannot discriminate based on protected classes (race, color, religion, sex, national origin, disability, familial status)

What to Look For

Focus on criminal history related to property damage, violence, theft, drug dealing, or other serious crimes. Minor or dated offenses should be evaluated in context with other application information and evidence of rehabilitation.

Step 5: Employment and Income Verification

Beyond reviewing pay stubs, verify employment details directly:

  • Contact the employer's HR or payroll department
  • Confirm the position title and salary information
  • Ask about employment status and likelihood of continued employment
  • For self-employed applicants, verify business licenses and references from business associates

Step 6: Personal References

Contact 2-3 personal references (non-family) that the applicant provides. Ask about their reliability, character, and ability to pay obligations. While these references are naturally biased, inconsistencies or unwillingness to speak positively can be revealing.

Fair Housing Compliance in Screening

This is crucial: ensure your screening process complies with fair housing laws. The Fair Housing Act prohibits discrimination based on:

  • Race or color
  • Religion
  • Sex (including gender identity)
  • National origin
  • Familial status (presence of children)
  • Disability
  • Sexual orientation (protected in many states)
Critical Rule: Apply the same screening criteria consistently to all applicants. Do not ask different questions, use different standards, or require different documentation based on protected characteristics.

Establishing Screening Standards

Document your screening standards in writing before you receive any applications. This helps ensure consistent, fair evaluation:

  • Minimum income requirement (e.g., 30x monthly rent annually)
  • Minimum credit score (if using one)
  • Allowable eviction history (e.g., no evictions in last 5 years)
  • Allowable criminal history (if any) with specific considerations
  • Verification requirements (pay stubs, references, employment check)
  • How you'll evaluate borderline applicants

Common Screening Mistakes to Avoid

Protect yourself legally by avoiding these common pitfalls:

  • Inconsistent standards: Applying different standards to different applicants
  • Incomplete documentation: Failing to document your screening process and decisions
  • Informal screening: Using word-of-mouth or gut feelings instead of objective criteria
  • Asking illegal questions: Inquiring about age, disability, family planning, or other protected information
  • Denying without explanation: Not providing reasons for rejection
  • Using outdated information: Relying on old eviction or criminal records without considering context

Technology and Screening Services

Modern tenant screening services streamline the process significantly. These services typically provide:

  • Online application forms
  • Credit reports and score analysis
  • Eviction history checks
  • Criminal background screening
  • Fraud detection (fake SSN, duplicate applications)
  • Employment and income verification
  • Complete screening reports with recommendations
