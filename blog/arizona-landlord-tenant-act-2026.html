Understanding the Arizona Landlord-Tenant Act: What Property Owners Need to Know in 2026

June 11, 2026 • 8 min read

Whether you're a first-time landlord in Phoenix or a seasoned real estate investor with properties across the Valley, understanding Arizona's landlord-tenant laws is essential for protecting your investment and maintaining positive tenant relationships. The Arizona Residential Landlord and Tenant Act (A.R.S. Title 33, Chapter 10) governs the rights and responsibilities of both parties in residential lease agreements.

In this comprehensive guide, we'll break down the key provisions of Arizona's landlord-tenant laws that every Phoenix property owner should know in 2026.

Security Deposits in Arizona

One of the most common areas of confusion for landlords involves security deposit regulations. Arizona law sets clear guidelines:

  • Maximum Amount: Arizona does not limit the amount you can charge for a security deposit. However, charging more than one and a half months' rent may make your property less competitive.
  • Refund Timeline: You must return the security deposit within 14 days after the tenant moves out and returns the keys.
  • Itemized Deductions: If you withhold any portion of the deposit, you must provide an itemized statement of deductions within the 14-day window.
  • Allowable Deductions: You may deduct for unpaid rent, cleaning costs to return the unit to its original condition (beyond normal wear and tear), and damages caused by the tenant.
Pro Tip: Always conduct a thorough move-in inspection with your tenant and document the property's condition with dated photos. This protects you in any deposit dispute.

Lease Agreement Requirements

While verbal rental agreements are technically legal in Arizona, written leases are strongly recommended. A comprehensive lease should include:

  • Names of all tenants and landlord
  • Property address and description
  • Lease term (start and end dates)
  • Rent amount, due date, and acceptable payment methods
  • Security deposit amount and terms
  • Late fee policies
  • Maintenance responsibilities
  • Pet policies (if applicable)
  • Rules regarding noise, parking, and common areas

Landlord Responsibilities

Arizona law requires landlords to maintain their rental properties in a habitable condition. This includes:

Essential Services

  • Providing running water and reasonable amounts of hot water
  • Maintaining heating and cooling systems in working order
  • Providing electricity and plumbing in good condition
  • Ensuring the property is weatherproof and free from pests

Safety Requirements

  • Installing working smoke detectors
  • Providing proper locks on doors and windows
  • Maintaining common areas in safe condition
  • Addressing any conditions that materially affect health and safety

The Eviction Process in Arizona

Arizona is considered a landlord-friendly state with relatively streamlined eviction procedures. However, you must follow the legal process carefully:

For Non-Payment of Rent

  1. 5-Day Notice: Serve a written notice giving the tenant 5 days to pay rent or vacate
  2. File for Eviction: If the tenant doesn't pay or leave, file a forcible detainer action in Justice Court
  3. Court Hearing: Typically scheduled within 3-6 days of filing
  4. Writ of Restitution: If you win, the court issues a writ allowing the constable to remove the tenant

For Lease Violations

  • Material Health/Safety Violations: 5-day notice with no opportunity to cure for certain serious violations
  • Other Violations: 10-day notice giving tenant opportunity to remedy the violation
Important: Never attempt "self-help" evictions such as changing locks, removing doors, or shutting off utilities. These actions are illegal and can result in significant liability.

Tenant Rights You Must Respect

To avoid legal issues, landlords should be aware of tenant rights under Arizona law:

  • Right to Privacy: You must provide at least 2 days' notice before entering the property, except in emergencies
  • Right to Habitable Conditions: Tenants can request repairs and, in certain circumstances, withhold rent or make repairs themselves
  • Protection from Retaliation: You cannot raise rent, decrease services, or threaten eviction in response to a tenant exercising their legal rights
  • Fair Housing Protections: You cannot discriminate based on race, color, religion, sex, familial status, national origin, or disability

Recent Updates for 2026

Several legislative changes have impacted Arizona landlord-tenant law in recent years. Stay current on:

  • Updated fair housing regulations
  • Changes to late fee limitations in certain municipalities
  • New disclosure requirements for certain property conditions
  • Updated procedures for abandoned property
