No landlord enjoys evicting a tenant, but knowing how the process works in Arizona is one of the best ways to protect your investment when a tenancy goes wrong. Arizona has one of the faster eviction timelines in the country, but only if you follow each step precisely. A single defective notice can send you back to square one and cost you weeks of lost rent.

This guide walks through the eviction process under the Arizona Residential Landlord and Tenant Act (Arizona Revised Statutes Title 33, Chapter 10). It is general information, not legal advice, so always confirm the specifics of your situation with a qualified Arizona attorney before filing.

Step 1: Identify the Reason and Serve the Correct Notice

Arizona does not allow you to remove a tenant without cause during a lease term, and you cannot use "self-help" methods such as changing the locks, removing doors, or shutting off utilities. Doing so exposes you to significant tenant damages. Instead, the process always begins with the correct written notice, and the notice depends on the reason.

Nonpayment of Rent: 5-Day Notice

If a tenant fails to pay rent, you must serve a written 5-day notice demanding payment. If the tenant pays the full amount owed (plus any late fees stated in the lease) within five days, the tenancy continues. If they do not, you may file for eviction.

Lease Violations and Health/Safety Issues: 10-Day or 5-Day Notice

For a curable lease violation, such as an unauthorized pet or occupant, you serve a 10-day notice giving the tenant the chance to fix the problem. For a material health or safety violation, the notice period is 5 days.

Serious Misconduct: Immediate Notice

For material and irreparable breaches, such as a tenant committing a serious crime on the property, discharging a weapon, or engaging in certain illegal drug activity, Arizona allows an immediate notice with no cure period.

Documentation matters: Keep proof of how and when every notice was delivered. Arizona allows delivery in person, by certified mail, or by posting in a conspicuous place. Photograph posted notices with a timestamp.

Step 2: File a Special Detainer Action

If the notice period passes and the tenant has not paid or cured the violation, you file a "special detainer" action in the justice court for the precinct where the property is located. You will need a copy of the lease, the notice you served, proof of service, and an itemized ledger of what is owed. There is a filing fee, plus a fee to have the tenant served with the summons and complaint.

Step 3: The Court Hearing

Arizona moves quickly. The court typically schedules the hearing within a short window after filing, often just a few days. Both parties appear before a justice of the peace. As the landlord, you carry the burden of proving the tenant violated the lease or failed to pay, so bring complete documentation.

If the tenant does not appear, the court usually enters a default judgment in your favor. If they do appear and raise a defense, such as claiming you failed to maintain the property, the judge will weigh the evidence. This is exactly why thorough records of maintenance requests and responses are so valuable.

Step 4: Judgment and the Writ of Restitution

If you win, the court issues a judgment that may include back rent, late fees, court costs, and attorney's fees if your lease provides for them. The tenant generally has a short period to vacate. If they still do not leave, you request a writ of restitution, which authorizes the constable to remove the tenant. Only the constable, not you, may carry out the physical removal.

Step 5: Handling the Tenant's Property

After a tenant is removed, Arizona law sets specific rules for handling any personal property left behind, including holding it for a defined period and notifying the tenant. Mishandling abandoned property is a common and avoidable source of liability, so follow the statute carefully.

How Long Does It Take?

When everything goes smoothly, an Arizona eviction for nonpayment can move from notice to writ in a matter of weeks. Delays usually come from defective notices, incomplete paperwork, or improper service, all of which are preventable with careful preparation.

The Better Strategy: Avoid Eviction Altogether

Eviction is expensive even when you win, between lost rent, court costs, and turnover. The most effective protection is on the front end:

  • Screen thoroughly: Verified income, credit, background, and rental history dramatically reduce the odds of nonpayment.
  • Document everything: A clear lease, signed move-in inspection, and written records of all communication strengthen your position if a dispute arises.
  • Act early: Serve the 5-day notice promptly when rent is late. Waiting "to be nice" usually makes the eventual outcome worse for everyone.
  • Communicate: Sometimes a payment plan or a cash-for-keys arrangement resolves the situation faster and cheaper than a contested hearing.
