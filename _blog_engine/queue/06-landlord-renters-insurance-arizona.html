Insurance is one of those topics landlords tend to set up once and never revisit, which is exactly how coverage gaps go unnoticed until a claim is denied. If you own a rental in Arizona, it is worth understanding the difference between the policies that protect you and the policies that protect your tenants, and why requiring the right coverage from both sides is simply good risk management.

Why a Homeowner's Policy Isn't Enough

Many new landlords assume the homeowner's policy they had while living in the home still protects them once it becomes a rental. Often it does not. Insurers price and underwrite owner-occupied homes differently from rentals, and a standard homeowner's policy may exclude or limit coverage once the property is tenant-occupied. If you have converted a former residence into a rental, that is a conversation to have with your agent right away.

Common gap: Filing a claim on a tenant-occupied home under an owner-occupied policy can lead to a denial. Make sure your policy reflects how the property is actually used.

What Landlord (Dwelling) Insurance Typically Covers

A landlord or "dwelling" policy is designed for rental properties and generally addresses three areas:

  • The structure: Coverage for the building itself against covered perils such as fire or certain storm damage. In Arizona, pay attention to how wind, dust storms, and monsoon-related damage are treated.
  • Liability: Protection if someone is injured on the property and you are found responsible, which can be one of the most financially significant risks you face.
  • Loss of rental income: Coverage that can replace rent if the home becomes uninhabitable due to a covered loss while it is repaired.

Optional add-ons worth discussing with your agent may include coverage for appliances and systems, and higher liability limits or an umbrella policy if you own multiple properties.

Why You Should Require Renters Insurance

Your landlord policy covers the building, not your tenant's belongings or their liability. That is what renters insurance is for, and requiring it in the lease benefits you as much as the tenant. Renters insurance typically provides:

  • Personal property coverage for the tenant's belongings, so they are not looking to you after a loss
  • Tenant liability coverage, which can respond if the tenant causes damage, for example a kitchen fire or an overflow, potentially protecting your property and reducing disputes
  • Additional living expenses if the tenant is temporarily displaced

Requiring renters insurance reduces the odds that a tenant's loss becomes your problem, and it tends to attract more responsible tenants. The cost to the tenant is usually modest, often a small monthly amount, which makes it an easy and reasonable lease requirement.

How to Require It Properly

  • Put it in the lease: State the requirement clearly, including any minimum liability coverage you want the tenant to carry.
  • Ask to be named as an interested party: This lets you receive notice if the policy lapses or is canceled.
  • Verify at move-in and renewal: Collect proof of coverage before handing over keys and confirm it remains in force.

A Few Arizona-Specific Considerations

Arizona's climate creates particular risks worth reviewing with your agent: monsoon wind and water damage, dust storms, occasional flooding in certain areas, and heat-related stress on roofs and systems. Flood damage in particular is often excluded from standard policies and may require separate coverage depending on the property's location. It is worth confirming exactly how your policy treats these regional perils rather than assuming.
