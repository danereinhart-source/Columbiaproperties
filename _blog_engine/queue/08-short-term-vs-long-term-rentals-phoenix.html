With Phoenix and Scottsdale among the most popular vacation and event destinations in the Southwest, many owners wonder whether they would earn more by running a short-term rental than a traditional lease. The honest answer is: it depends on the property, your goals, and how much involvement you want. Here is a clear-eyed comparison to help you decide.

The Basic Tradeoff

Short-term rentals (think nightly or weekly stays booked through platforms like Airbnb or Vrbo) can generate higher gross revenue per night, especially during peak season and major events. Long-term rentals (leases of a year or more) generate lower headline rent but far more stability and far less work. Most of the differences below flow from that core tradeoff between income potential and consistency.

Income: Higher Ceiling vs. Higher Floor

Phoenix has strong seasonal demand. In the cooler months, during spring training, and around major sporting and golf events, nightly rates can spike and a well-run short-term rental can outperform a long-term lease. But that income is uneven. Summer demand drops sharply in the desert heat, and occupancy can fall with it.

A long-term lease trades that upside for a dependable monthly check, twelve months a year, regardless of season. It is a higher floor with a lower ceiling.

Reality check: Compare net income, not gross. Short-term rentals carry costs long-term rentals do not, and a high nightly rate at 55% annual occupancy can net less than a steady lease once expenses are counted.

Costs and Effort

This is where the two strategies diverge most. A short-term rental is effectively a small hospitality business:

  • Furnishing: The home must be fully furnished and equipped, a significant upfront cost.
  • Turnover: Cleaning and restocking between every stay, often multiple times a week.
  • Utilities and supplies: You pay electricity (no small thing with Arizona summer cooling), internet, and consumables.
  • Guest communication: Bookings, questions, check-ins, and the occasional problem, sometimes at all hours.
  • Higher wear: Frequent guests and constant use accelerate wear on the home.

A long-term tenant, by contrast, typically pays their own utilities, stays for a year or more, and handles day-to-day living without your involvement. The management effort is a fraction of a nightly rental's.

Regulations: Do Your Homework First

This is the step too many owners skip. Short-term rentals in Arizona are subject to state and local rules that have evolved significantly, and individual cities have added their own requirements such as licensing or permitting, taxes, safety standards, and operational rules. On top of that, many HOAs and condo associations restrict or prohibit short-term rentals entirely.

Before counting on short-term income, verify:

  • Current state and city regulations for the specific municipality where your property sits
  • Any required licenses, permits, or registrations and the applicable taxes
  • Your HOA or condo rules, which can override your plans regardless of city law

Because these rules change and vary by location, confirm the current requirements for your exact address rather than relying on what was true a year or two ago.

Risk and Vacancy Profile

Long-term rentals concentrate risk in a single tenant: if they stop paying, your income stops until the situation is resolved. Short-term rentals spread income across many guests but are exposed to seasonality, platform algorithm changes, and economic swings in travel. Neither is risk-free; they are just different risks.

Which Should You Choose?

A short-term strategy may make sense if your property is in a high-demand location (near Old Town Scottsdale, event venues, or desirable vacation areas), you can absorb seasonal swings, the local rules and HOA permit it, and you are comfortable running a hospitality operation or paying someone to. A long-term strategy is usually the better fit if you value predictable income, lower effort, and simpler compliance, which describes most rental owners.

Some owners even blend approaches over the year, though that adds complexity and regulatory exposure.
