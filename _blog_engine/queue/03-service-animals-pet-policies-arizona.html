Few areas of property management trip up landlords more than the difference between a pet and an assistance animal. Get it wrong and you risk a fair housing complaint that can be far more costly than any pet damage. This guide explains how Arizona landlords can set sensible pet policies while staying on the right side of federal Fair Housing law.

Pets vs. Assistance Animals: A Critical Distinction

Under the federal Fair Housing Act, assistance animals are not pets, and your pet policy does not apply to them. Assistance animals fall into two categories:

  • Service animals: Typically dogs individually trained to perform a task for a person with a disability, such as guiding someone who is blind or alerting to a medical event.
  • Assistance/support animals (including emotional support animals): Animals that provide disability-related support but may not have specialized training.

Both are treated as a reasonable accommodation request, not a pet. That has major practical consequences for how you handle them.

Key rule: You generally cannot charge pet rent, pet fees, or a pet deposit for a legitimate assistance animal, and breed or weight restrictions in your pet policy do not apply to them. You may, however, hold the tenant responsible for any actual damage the animal causes.

Setting a Compliant Pet Policy

For actual pets, Arizona landlords have wide latitude. A clear written pet policy in your lease should address:

  • Whether pets are allowed and any limits on number, size, or type
  • Pet rent or fees: A monthly pet rent and/or a refundable pet deposit are both common and permissible for pets
  • Documentation: Vaccination records, and a description or photo of the animal
  • Responsibility: Language making the tenant liable for any pet-related damage and for nuisance issues like excessive noise

Consistency is essential. Apply your pet policy the same way for every applicant to avoid any appearance of discrimination.

Handling an Assistance Animal Request

When a tenant or applicant requests to keep an assistance animal, treat it as a reasonable accommodation request. Here is the general framework:

What You Can Ask For

If the person's disability or the need for the animal is not obvious, you may request reliable documentation that (1) the person has a disability and (2) the animal provides disability-related assistance. For a person with a visible disability and an obvious service animal, you generally should not ask for documentation at all.

What You Cannot Do

  • You cannot require the animal to have special certification or training credentials.
  • You cannot ask about the nature or severity of the person's disability.
  • You cannot charge pet fees, pet rent, or a pet deposit for the assistance animal.
  • You cannot apply breed or weight restrictions to deny it.

When You May Be Able to Deny

Accommodation is not unlimited. A request may be denied if the specific animal poses a direct threat to the health or safety of others that cannot be reduced by another accommodation, or would cause substantial physical damage to property, based on actual evidence rather than stereotypes about a breed. These are narrow exceptions, so document your reasoning carefully and consider legal counsel before denying.

Beware of Fraudulent Documentation

Online "ESA certificates" have made this area messier. While some requests are not legitimate, reacting by refusing all assistance animals or demanding improper documentation is exactly what leads to liability. The safer path is a consistent, lawful verification process applied to every request, rather than snap judgments.

Why This Matters for Your Investment

Fair housing complaints are time-consuming and expensive, and "I didn't know" is not a defense. At the same time, a thoughtful pet policy can actually expand your applicant pool and reduce vacancy, since a large share of renters have pets and many will pay a premium for pet-friendly homes.
